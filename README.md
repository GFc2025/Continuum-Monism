Continuum Monism (CM)
A structural framework for understanding sovereignty, fragility, and shared reality.

Continuum Monism (CM) is a worldview and analytical lens built around a simple idea:

> All experiences, beliefs, and perspectives exist within a single continuum.  
> Shared reality is the overlap between individual continuums.  
> Sovereignty and fragility determine how that overlap behaves.

CM is not a doctrine, ideology, or moral system.  
It is a map — a way to understand how minds interact, how conflicts arise, and how shared reality can be stabilized without domination or dogma.

CM is designed to be:

- inclusive (all beliefs exist in the continuum)  
- non‑authoritarian (no one’s map overrides another’s by default)  
- structural (based on sovereignty, not morality)  
- scalable (works for individuals, pairs, groups, and societies)  
- collaborative (open to refinement, extension, and critique)

This repository contains the core axioms, diagrams, ethical layer, and examples that make CM a usable, testable framework.

---

Why CM Exists

CM was created to answer a simple but difficult question:

How can multiple minds coexist without collapsing each other’s sovereignty?

The answer CM offers is structural, not moral:

- Protect fragile sovereignty  
- Preserve the shared intersection  
- Expand acceptable belief‑space  
- Reduce collapse points  
- Maintain autonomy without domination  

CM is a tool for navigating conflict, meaning, identity, and coexistence in a way that respects both individuality and connection.

---

Core Principles

1. The Continuum
Everything experienced — thoughts, beliefs, sensations, memories, perceptions — exists within a single continuum.  
Nothing is “outside” it.

2. Sovereignty
Each mind‑body system has autonomy over its own internal map and embodied experience.  
Sovereignty is not granted — it is inherent.

3. The Shared Intersection
Shared reality is the overlap between continuums.  
It is fragile, negotiated, and maintained through communication and consent.

4. The Fragility Principle
When sovereignties conflict, the more fragile sovereignty should be protected,  
as long as doing so does not collapse the autonomy or existence of the less fragile one.

This is not moral heroism — it is structural stability.

5. Acceptable Belief‑Space
All beliefs are acceptable as internal maps.  
Only actions that collapse another’s sovereignty or the shared intersection are unacceptable.

---

What CM Is Not

- Not a religion  
- Not a political ideology  
- Not a moral system  
- Not a universal truth claim  
- Not a replacement for science, law, or ethics  

CM is a lens — a way to analyze sovereignty, conflict, and coexistence.

---

Relationship to the Giving‑First Charter

The Giving‑First Charter describes a behavioral posture:  
how individuals can act generously, cooperatively, and with dignity.

CM describes a structural posture:  
how sovereignty, fragility, and shared reality interact.

Together, they form a coherent worldview:

- CM explains why sovereignty matters.  
- Giving‑First explains how to act within that sovereignty.  

They are independent but mutually reinforcing.
